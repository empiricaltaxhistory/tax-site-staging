Appendix A: Methodology

This study employs a consistent empirical framework to evaluate behavioral responses to major statutory and structural changes in federal income-tax policy from 1913 to 2000, a period in which continuous and internally consistent data permit meaningful cross-era comparison. Earlier episodes (1861–1872 and 1894–1895) are addressed briefly to provide the constitutional and philosophical foundation for the modern system. Because statutory definitions, periods of persistent inflation, administrative practices, and data reporting standards change across time, the analysis relies on a unified set of conventions designed to maximize comparability while maintaining fidelity to the underlying sources. These conventions are described below.

1. Inflation Adjustments

All nominal values are converted to constant 2017 dollars using official Bureau of Labor Statistics series. For 1913 through 1976 the analysis uses the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U), and for 1977 onward the analysis relies on the Consumer Price Index Research Series (CPI-U-RS). CPI-U-RS is chosen for the post-1977 period because it provides a backward-consistent index that incorporates modern methodological improvements. Using the CPI-U/CPI-U-RS combination preserves comparability while avoiding discontinuities introduced by post-hoc revisions.

All deflation is performed on a calendar-year basis. Where IRS Statistics of Income (SOI) data are published in current dollars, income amounts and revenue aggregates are converted to constant 2017 dollars. Statutory bracket thresholds are shown in real terms only for comparison across periods and do not represent indexed brackets prior to 1985. Throughout the manuscript, whole-numbered tables report values in nominal dollars, while decimal-numbered tables report the same series in constant 2017 dollars.

Macroeconomic aggregates reported by the Office of Management and Budget (OMB), including revenues, outlays, and debt, are expressed using GDP price indices rather than CPI-based deflators and are reported on a fiscal-year basis. Where GDP-deflated series are used, they are presented as shares of gross domestic product rather than converted to CPI-adjusted dollar levels. As a result, real-dollar values derived from GDP-deflated sources differ modestly from CPI-U or CPI-U-RS conversions, although comparative trends and relationships are unaffected.

2. Primary Tax Data Sources

The principal source for taxpayer behavior is the Internal Revenue Service Statistics of Income (SOI), including Individual Income Tax Returns, Net Income tables, Adjusted Gross Income tables, Revenue tables, and Historical Table 1 where applicable. The analysis relies exclusively on tables published contemporaneously or in the IRS’s annual compilations, without reconstruction or retroactive adjustment.

Because SOI data reflect reported taxable income rather than total economic income, the analysis treats SOI values as behavioral indicators. Reported income rises or falls in response to incentives, definitions, and enforcement environments; it does not directly measure underlying economic inequality. This distinction is fundamental to the interpretive framework of the manuscript.

3. Income Definitions and Comparability

Across the century covered here, income definitions change substantially. Until 1943, the IRS reported Net Income After Credits, which differs from post-1944 Adjusted Gross Income (AGI). The Revenue Act of 1944 introduced AGI and reorganized the tax base accordingly. Later reforms further complicate comparisons.

The Tax Reform Act of 1986 represents a major definitional break, eliminating numerous shelters, limiting passive losses, altering depreciation rules, and reclassifying certain categories of income. As a result, AGI values reported prior to 1986 are not directly comparable to AGI values after 1986. This study treats TRA86 as the single most important measurement discontinuity in the postwar series and interprets post-1986 increases in high-income AGI with appropriate caution.

No attempt is made to create a synthetic harmonized series; instead, discontinuities are acknowledged explicitly where they affect interpretation.

4. Tax-Rate Data

Statutory rates are drawn from the Treasury Department’s “Individual Income Tax Rates and Tax Shares” and accompanying Historical Rate Schedules. These schedules show normal tax, surtax, exemptions, bracket thresholds, and marginal rates. Because statutory rates differ from effective burdens, the analysis treats rate schedules as indicators of marginal incentive structures, not as summaries of actual tax payments.

All comparisons involving rate changes are grounded in statutory legislation, which is identified where comprehensive changes to the tax code are enacted. Throughout the manuscript, references to “tax cuts” denote statutory reductions in marginal tax rates. They do not imply reductions in aggregate liabilities or receipts, which depend on income composition, behavioral response, and macroeconomic conditions.

5. Revenue, Spending, and Macroeconomic Data

Macroeconomic aggregates—receipts, outlays, deficits, debt, unemployment, real GDP growth, and inflation—are taken from the Office of Management and Budget Historical Tables, the Bureau of Economic Analysis National Income and Product Accounts, and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. All fiscal aggregates are expressed as shares of gross domestic product where possible to permit consistent comparison across eras of differing nominal scales, population sizes, and price levels.

Because OMB tables are reported on a fiscal-year basis, while SOI data reflect calendar years, the analysis treats each series within its own temporal conventions without attempt to force artificial alignment. Historical GDP series prior to the availability of modern BEA tables draw on BEA reconstructions and Measuring Worth where necessary; these series use GDP price indices rather than CPI deflators.

6. Behavioral Interpretation

The analysis views taxpayer responses through the lens of behavioral economics and public-finance research. Because individuals optimize along the margins defined by statutory rates, deductions, exemptions, and shelter availability, reported income is treated as a function of the incentive environment.

This approach is consistent with empirical literature on the elasticity of reported income (ETI), which captures how taxable income responds to changes in marginal rates. ETI-based reasoning is used here to interpret movements in high-income reporting across the century, but the manuscript does not assign a fixed elasticity parameter. Instead, it draws on observed changes in SOI values.

Capital gains realizations are treated as behavioral responses to statutory rate changes rather than as measures of underlying wealth accumulation. Spikes and collapses in reported gains are interpreted as timing effects unless corroborated by sustained changes in income shares or receipts.

7. Treatment of Non-Filers, Base Erosion, and Tax Units

SOI data reflect tax units that file returns; non-filers do not appear. In periods where exemptions rise substantially (notably 1925–1931), millions of low-income individuals cease filing, reducing the count of returns but not the underlying population. This manuscript estimates the magnitude of “missing filers” where relevant but does not impute income to non-filers.

The analysis instead focuses on documented changes within the filing population. Patterns of base erosion, bracket creep, and shelter use are discussed where statutory or inflationary conditions encourage them. The term “tax unit” refers to a filing return as recorded in SOI data; “taxpayer” or “earner” is used only when describing behavioral decision-making rather than accounting outcomes.

8. Legislative and Institutional Context

Because tax rules evolve within a constitutional and institutional framework, this study incorporates legislative history from presidential messages, congressional debates, committee reports, voting results, and statutory language. The analysis respects the constitutional allocation of revenue-raising authority to the House of Representatives and situates tax enactments within the political alignments of each period.

This contextualization is descriptive rather than normative; the manuscript avoids attributing motive except where documentary evidence clearly supports it.

9. Scope and Limitations

The study primarily limits itself to federal income tax behavior and does not evaluate payroll taxes, state and local taxation, or wealth effects except where these intersect directly with federal incentives. Corporate, state, and local taxes are noted where material. The analysis does not attempt to measure “true” economic inequality; the manuscript’s claims are confined to what the federal income-tax system records.

Causal claims are made cautiously, with explicit recognition that macroeconomic outcomes result from interacting monetary, fiscal, demographic, and external factors. When attribution is uncertain, the manuscript states so explicitly.

10. Replicability

All data used in this study are publicly available from IRS SOI, OMB, BEA, and BLS. Spreadsheet formulas for deflation, percentage changes, and series alignment use standard arithmetic procedures documented directly in the manuscript's tables. The conversion methods, deflators, and comparability notes provided here are sufficient for replicating every table and figure in the manuscript.

Much of the historical IRS Statistics of Income data used in this study—particularly for the period prior to mid-century digitization—required manual extraction from original Bureau of Internal Revenue and IRS publications. Appendix B reproduces these primary tables in their original published format to preserve contemporaneous definitions, reporting conventions, and internal structure, and to allow independent verification without the risk of transcription or normalization drift.
